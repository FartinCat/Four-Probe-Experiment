Why Four Probes?

In a two-probe measurement, contact resistance adds to the measured resistance — a large error in semiconductors. The Four-Probe Method fixes this by separating current injection from voltage sensing:

  • Outer probes P1 & P4 — carry the current I.
  • Inner probes P2 & P3 — measure voltage V. No current flows through them, so contact resistance does not affect the reading.
Resistivity Formula

For a thin specimen (W/s < 0.5 — our case: W/s = 0.25):

ρ = (V / I) × (π × W / ln 2)
ρ in Ω·cm | W in cm | V in Volts | I in Amperes
Temperature Dependence & Band Gap
ρ(T) = ρ₀ · exp(Eg / 2kT)
Eg = band gap (eV) | k = 8.617×10⁻⁵ eV/K | T in Kelvin

Taking logarithm → ln(ρ) vs 1/T is a straight line. Slope = Eg/(2k):

Eg = 2 × k × |slope| = 2 × 8.617×10⁻⁵ × |slope| eV

Instruments Required
  • Four-Probe Apparatus (spring-loaded tungsten probe unit)

  • Digital Milliammeter (resolution: 0.01 mA)
  • Type-K Thermocouple with digital temperature indicator (±0.5°C)

Circuit Description
  • Current Loop: CCS → P1 → Silicon specimen → P4 → back to CCS.
  • Voltage Loop: Digital Millivoltmeter across P2 and P3 (inner probes only).
⚠ Probes must be collinear: P1 – P2 – P3 – P4, with equal spacing s between each adjacent pair.
Step-by-Step Instructions
1
Sample Preparation

Clean the Silicon specimen with acetone/IPA. Mount it flat on the insulated sample holder.

2
Probe Alignment & Contact

Lower the four collinear probes (s = 0.20 cm) gently using the micrometer. Apply minimum pressure for stable electrical contact.

⚠ Probe order: P1 – P2 – P3 – P4 (left to right). Verify equal spacing.
3
Circuit Connections

Connect outer probes P1 & P4 to the Constant Current Source. Connect inner probes P2 & P3 to the Digital Millivoltmeter.

📌 Never connect the current source to inner probes or voltmeter to outer probes.
4
Thermocouple Installation

Insert the thermocouple into the sample holder as close to the specimen as possible. Verify room temperature on the indicator.

5
Room Temperature Reading

Set I = 5 mA. Record T₁ (≈ 27°C) and V₁ from the millivoltmeter. This is Observation No. 1.

6
Heating & Data Collection

Switch on the oven. Increase temperature in steps of ~10°C. Wait ~2 min at each step for stabilisation, then record T and V.

📌 Keep I constant at 5 mA throughout. Do not change it between readings.
7
Maximum Temperature

Continue until ~175°C (448 K). Take at least 14–16 readings total.

⚠ Do not exceed 200°C — permanent specimen damage may occur.
8
Cooling Down

Switch off the heater after the last reading. Let the system cool naturally before removing the specimen.

9
Calculations & Graph

Compute R = V/I, ρ = R·πW/ln2, ln(ρ) for each row. Plot ln(ρ) vs 1/T to find slope → Eg.

How to Read the Graph
  • Each black dot is one observation: x = 1/T × 10³, y = ln(ρ).
  • The red dashed line is the best-fit linear regression.
  • Slope of the line = Eg / (2k). Read from graph or the value shown below canvas.
  • Eg = 2 × k × |slope| = 2 × 8.617×10⁻⁵ × |slope_K| eV
  • For p-Si the slope ≈ 6500 K → Eg ≈ 1.12 eV.

Conclusion

The resistivity of the Silicon (p-type) specimen decreases exponentially with increasing temperature, confirming semiconductor behaviour. From the slope of the ln(ρ) vs 1/T graph, the band gap energy of Silicon is determined — expected to be close to the standard value of 1.12 eV.

Precautions
  1. Ensure the four probes are collinear and equally spaced (s = 0.20 cm).
  2. Apply minimum pressure to the probes — excessive force cracks the chip.
  3. Always pass current through outer probes (P1, P4) and measure voltage across inner probes (P2, P3).
  4. Keep the current constant at I = 5 mA throughout; do not adjust between readings.
  5. Allow temperature to stabilise (~2 min) at each step before recording.
  6. Do not exceed 200°C — specimen may be permanently damaged.
  7. Use a regulated DC constant current source.
  8. Ensure good thermal contact between thermocouple and specimen holder.
  9. Avoid vibrations during measurements.
  10. Take at least 14–16 readings for a reliable regression.
Sources of Error
  • Non-uniform probe contact pressure — variable contact resistance.
  • Thermal lag between heater and specimen — stabilisation time too short.
  • Slightly unequal probe spacing.
  • Electromagnetic interference on millivoltmeter leads.
  • Oxide layer or surface contamination on the specimen.
  • Current source drift over time.
