Why Four Probes?

In a conventional two-probe measurement the contact resistance between probe tips and the specimen adds to the measured resistance, introducing large systematic errors — especially problematic in semiconductors where bulk resistance can be comparable to contact resistance.

The Four-Probe Method eliminates this error by separating current injection from voltage sensing:

  • Outer probes (P1 & P4) carry the current I.
  • Inner probes (P2 & P3) measure voltage V — no current flows through them, so their contact resistance does not affect the reading.
Resistivity Formula

For a semiconductor specimen of thickness W with equally spaced collinear probes (spacing s), the resistivity is:

ρ = (V / I) × 2πs × f(W/s)
f(W/s) = geometrical correction factor

For a thin specimen (W ≪ s, i.e., W/s < 0.5), the formula simplifies to:

ρ = (V / I) × (π W / ln 2)
ρ in Ω·cm | W in cm | V in Volts | I in Amperes

This lab uses: W = 0.05 cm, s = 0.20 cm → W/s = 0.25 ✓ (thin-sample regime)

Temperature Dependence & Band Gap

For an intrinsic semiconductor, resistivity varies exponentially with temperature:

ρ(T) = ρ₀ · exp(Eg / 2kT)
Eg = band gap energy (eV) | k = 8.617×10⁻⁵ eV/K | T = absolute temperature (K)

Taking the natural logarithm:

ln(ρ) = ln(ρ₀) + (Eg / 2k) · (1/T)

A plot of ln(ρ) versus 1/T is a straight line with:

  • Slope = Eg / (2k)
  • Therefore: Eg = 2k × |slope|
Eg = 2 × 8.617×10⁻⁵ × |slope|  eV
where slope is in Kelvin (from ln(ρ) vs 1/T in K⁻¹)
